I was applying UX principles before I even knew they existed.

My journey in Computer Science and Technology began with assembling my first PC. I dove into motherboards, Intel versus Ryzen debates, and endless comparisons across dozens of sites.
Holding a machine I built myself felt like unlocking a new world.

With that PC, I explored everything: programming, Linux, networking, and data entry.
A year later, after upgrading an acquaintance’s outdated laptop from Windows-XP to Windows-10, people began seeing me as the go-to tech person.
Over time, the excitement faded, and that identity started to feel like a trap.

That was when I shifted toward design. Figma, color theory, and the Laws of UX reframed how I approached problems.
After three months of focused practice, a familiar sense of joy returned. It was the same satisfaction I used to feel designing project cover pages or building cardboard prototypes, like custom pen boxes or cable organizers for my PC.
I realized I had been using the F-pattern, the 60-30-10 rule, and thoughtful typography long before learning the theory. Suddenly, the 'why' behind my decisions became clear.

For the first time, finishing something didn't make me want to move on. It made me want to go further. I had found my Ikigai.
